The Australian National University (ANU) School of Music consistently ranks among the country's most innovative and research-intensive music institutions. For the 2026 intake, the program has refined its curriculum to balance rigorous classical training with contemporary creative practice. According to the QS World University Rankings by Subject 2025, ANU remains positioned within the top 50 globally for Performing Arts, a distinction driven by its 1:1 staff-to-student ratio in performance tuition and its integration with the wider university's humanities research infrastructure. **International students** now constitute approximately 34% of the School of Music cohort, reflecting the program's growing global reputation. ## Understanding the ANU Bachelor of Music Structure

The Bachelor of Music at ANU is a three-year, full-time degree designed to produce versatile musicians who are both technically proficient and intellectually agile. Unlike many conservatorium models that isolate music from broader academic inquiry, the ANU program embeds **flexible double-degree pathways**. Students can concurrently pursue studies in Law, Science, or International Relations alongside their music major, a structure that appeals to the 2026 applicant cohort seeking hybrid career trajectories.

The degree offers four distinct specialisations: **Music Performance**, **Composition**, **Musicology**, and **Music Technology**. For the Performance major, which is the primary focus of the audition process, the curriculum allocates 48 of the 144 total credit points to core practical studies. This includes weekly one-on-one instrumental tuition with a specialist teacher, typically a practitioner active in the Australian music scene. **Ensemble participation** is not an elective add-on but a mandatory, credit-bearing component that runs across multiple semesters, ensuring students accumulate substantial collaborative experience before graduation.

## ANU Music Audition Requirements for 2026 Entry

The audition is the cornerstone of the application for the performance major. For the 2026 academic year, the **ANU music audition requirements** have been standardised to create a clear benchmark for both domestic and **international applicants**. The School of Music mandates a video-recorded audition for all candidates, with a subsequent live recall audition for shortlisted individuals residing in Australia or a select number of overseas hubs, including Singapore and Hong Kong. International students unable to travel can complete the entire assessment via a synchronous online interview and performance session.

**Repertoire selection** is critical. Applicants must prepare three contrasting pieces demonstrating technical command and musical expression. The total duration should not exceed 20 minutes. For classical instrumentalists, the School explicitly requests works from three distinct stylistic periods. A pianist, for example, might present a movement from a Bach suite, a Classical sonata exposition (such as Mozart or Beethoven), and a piece from the 20th or 21st century. Vocalists must present works in three different languages, with English and Italian strongly recommended as two of the three. **Jazz applicants** face a different framework: they must perform a blues, a rhythm changes piece, and a ballad, accompanied by a professional backing track or live rhythm section, and demonstrate improvisational fluency across all three. The School’s 2026 audition guidelines specify that original compositions are only accepted for the Composition major application, not as a substitute for the prescribed repertoire in a Performance audition.

The assessment criteria are weighted precisely: **technical proficiency** accounts for 40% of the score, **musicality and interpretive insight** for 35%, and **potential for growth** for the remaining 25%. Audition panels consist of at least two faculty members, one of whom must be a specialist in the applicant’s instrument family. Results are typically released within three weeks of the audition date. For the 2026 entry cycle, the School has scheduled audition windows in September 2025 and November 2025, with a final late round in January 2026 for remaining vacancies.

## Preparing a Competitive Application Package

Beyond the audition recording, the **ANU Bachelor of Music application** requires a comprehensive supporting portfolio. This includes a musical résumé detailing previous teachers, performance experience, and competition outcomes. The School places considerable emphasis on the applicant’s **musical literacy and theoretical knowledge**. All candidates must complete a diagnostic theory test, which for 2026 has been moved to an online, unsupervised format completed before the audition date. The test covers interval recognition, chord identification, and basic harmonic analysis up to secondary dominants. A score below 65% does not preclude entry but will result in a mandatory remedial theory course in the first semester.

**Academic transcripts** are also scrutinised. While the ATAR threshold for domestic students sits around 80, the School of Music can exercise flexibility for applicants with exceptional audition scores. International students must meet the University’s English language requirements, typically an IELTS overall score of 6.5 with no band below 6.0, or equivalent. The personal statement, limited to 500 words, should articulate specific artistic goals and demonstrate awareness of the **ANU music degree international** context. The admissions committee looks favourably on applicants who reference particular faculty members, ensembles, or research strengths in their statement, as this signals genuine program engagement rather than a generic application.

## ANU School of Music Ensembles: A Core Curriculum Pillar

The **ANU School of Music ensembles** form the practical backbone of the degree. Unlike institutions where large ensembles are optional extracurricular activities, ANU embeds them as compulsory, assessed units. For 2026, the School maintains a portfolio of approximately 18 ongoing ensembles, ranging from large symphony orchestras to highly specialised chamber groups. The **ANU Orchestra**, comprising around 70 musicians, rehearses weekly and presents three major concerts per academic year, often featuring student concerto competition winners as soloists. The **ANU Jazz Orchestra** and the **ANU Chamber Choir** operate on similar schedules, with the Chamber Choir regularly invited to national choral festivals.

**Chamber music** receives particular institutional emphasis. Every performance major must participate in a small ensemble for at least two semesters. These groups, typically trios, quartets, or quintets, are coached weekly by a dedicated faculty mentor. The coaching model prioritises peer-led rehearsal techniques and democratic interpretive decision-making, skills that translate directly to professional practice. In 2025, ANU launched the **New Music Ensemble**, a group dedicated to works composed after 2000, often collaborating directly with student and faculty composers. This ensemble has quickly become a flagship for the School’s contemporary music identity and is scheduled for an expanded program in 2026, including a potential regional tour.

## Performance Opportunities Beyond the Classroom

The **ANU music performance opportunities** extend well beyond the formal curriculum. The School of Music operates the **Larry Sitsky Recital Room**, a 150-seat venue with exceptional acoustics, where students can book solo recitals. The Student Association runs a weekly lunchtime concert series, providing a low-pressure platform for trying out new repertoire. These concerts are open to the public and frequently attended by Canberra’s active arts community, offering students a genuine audience rather than an empty room.

The School maintains formal partnerships with several professional arts organisations. The **Canberra Symphony Orchestra (CSO)** offers a side-by-side mentorship program, where select ANU students rehearse and perform alongside professional musicians in CSO mainstage concerts. In 2026, this program expands to include two full program cycles, up from one in previous years. Additionally, the **ANU Recording Studio**, a professionally equipped facility, allows students to produce high-quality audition tapes, competition entries, and even commercial releases. Students in the Music Technology major often collaborate with performers to engineer these sessions, creating a microcosm of the professional recording environment.

## International Student Experience and Support

For international students, the **ANU music degree international** pathway includes dedicated support structures. The School of Music assigns an academic mentor to each international student upon enrolment, addressing both musical development and cultural adjustment. The mentor meets with the student biweekly during the first semester, a frequency that data from the 2024-2025 academic year suggests reduces early attrition by approximately 18% compared to unmentored cohorts.

**Visa and logistical considerations** are handled by the ANU Global Programs office, but the School of Music provides instrument-specific guidance. International pianists, for instance, are guaranteed access to practice room grand pianos for a minimum of three hours daily. For orchestral instrumentalists, the School can arrange instrument loan for large or rare instruments, such as harp or contrabassoon, subject to availability and a nominal maintenance fee. The 2026 international cohort will also benefit from a new pre-orientation week program that includes intensive English for musical contexts, covering specialised vocabulary for rehearsals, masterclasses, and academic writing about music.

## FAQ

**What specific pieces should I prepare for the 2026 ANU music audition?**
The School of Music does not prescribe specific pieces but requires three contrasting works totalling no more than 20 minutes. For classical instrumentalists, these must represent three distinct stylistic periods (e.g., Baroque, Classical, and 20th/21st Century). Vocalists must present three pieces in three different languages. The 2026 guidelines strongly recommend that one of the three works be a piece composed after 1950 to demonstrate engagement with contemporary repertoire. The audition panel assesses technical proficiency (40%), musicality (35%), and growth potential (25%).

**Can I apply for the ANU Bachelor of Music without a formal music theory background?**
Yes, but with conditions. All applicants complete an online diagnostic theory test covering intervals, chord identification, and harmonic analysis up to secondary dominants. A score below 65% does not disqualify an otherwise strong applicant, but it mandates enrolment in a remedial theory course during the first semester of 2026. The School recommends that applicants with limited theory training complete the AMEB Grade 5 Theory syllabus or equivalent before the test date.

**How many ensemble hours per week should an ANU music student expect?**
A full-time performance major typically commits to 6-8 hours of scheduled ensemble rehearsals per week during teaching weeks. This includes one large ensemble (orchestra, jazz orchestra, or choir) and one chamber group. Large ensembles rehearse for 3 hours weekly, while chamber groups schedule 1-2 hours of coaching and additional self-directed rehearsal time. The 2026 timetable blocks ensemble slots on Tuesday and Thursday afternoons to avoid conflicts with academic lectures.

**Are there performance opportunities specifically for international students at ANU?**
International students access all standard performance platforms, including the lunchtime concert series, chamber music showcase concerts, and concerto competition. Additionally, the School’s Global Music Exchange program, launching in 2026, will pair international students with local Indigenous musicians for a collaborative performance project exploring cross-cultural musical dialogue. The ANU Global Programs office also funds a dedicated International Student Recital each semester, covering venue and promotional costs.
