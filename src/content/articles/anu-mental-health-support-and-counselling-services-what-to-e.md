Navigating mental health support at university can feel overwhelming, but understanding the process before you begin makes a significant difference. In 2026, the Australian National University continues to expand its mental health services, with **over 8,500 counselling sessions** delivered annually through the ANU Counselling and Wellbeing team. A 2025 student experience survey indicated that **74% of ANU students** who accessed on-campus counselling reported improved coping strategies within three sessions. This guide walks you through exactly what to expect from your first appointment, how to book, what after-hours support exists, and how mental health intersects with your academic responsibilities.

## Understanding the ANU Mental Health Support Framework

The ANU Counselling and Wellbeing Centre operates on a **stepped-care model**, meaning the level of support is matched to your individual needs. When you first reach out, you are not automatically placed into long-term therapy. Instead, the team conducts an initial assessment to determine whether short-term counselling, group programs, specialist referrals, or community-based support best suits your situation.

The service is staffed by **registered psychologists, social workers, and mental health clinicians** with experience in university-specific challenges such as academic stress, perfectionism, adjustment difficulties, and cross-cultural transitions. Importantly, all services are **free and confidential** for currently enrolled ANU students. Your personal information and session content are protected under Australian privacy legislation, and details are not shared with academic staff or family members without your explicit written consent, except in situations involving serious risk of harm.

## How to Book Your First ANU Counselling Appointment

The **ANU counselling booking steps** are designed to be straightforward, though demand can mean waiting times fluctuate throughout the academic semester. Here is the step-by-step process as it operates in 2026.

First, you need to contact the ANU Counselling and Wellbeing Centre directly. The primary method is by **phone at 02 6125 2442**, available Monday to Friday from 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM. When you call, a trained intake officer will ask you a few brief questions about what brings you to counselling. This is not a therapy session; it is a triage conversation lasting approximately 10 to 15 minutes to help them understand your level of urgency and match you with an appropriate clinician.

Alternatively, you can **visit the Centre in person** on Level 3 of the Health and Wellbeing Centre in Kambri. In-person bookings follow the same triage process. For students who find phone calls difficult, the Centre also accepts **email enquiries**, though phone triage is generally required before a first appointment is confirmed. Once triaged, you will be offered an initial appointment, typically within **one to two weeks** for non-urgent matters, and sooner if your situation requires immediate attention.

## What Happens During Your First ANU Counselling Session

Walking into your **ANU counselling first appointment**, you might feel nervous or uncertain. That is entirely normal. The session usually runs for **50 minutes**, and the first portion is dedicated to understanding your current concerns, your personal history, and what you hope to gain from counselling.

Your clinician will begin by outlining the **confidentiality framework** and the limits of that confidentiality. They will explain that notes are kept securely and that counselling records are separate from your academic file. You will then be invited to share your story at your own pace. Common topics that arise in first appointments include academic pressure, relationship difficulties, anxiety, low mood, sleep disturbances, and concerns about the future. The clinician will ask questions to clarify your experiences, but there is no obligation to disclose anything you are not ready to share.

By the end of the session, you and your clinician will collaboratively develop a **support plan**. This might involve booking further counselling sessions, connecting you with a group workshop on stress management, or providing a referral to a GP for a mental health care plan if longer-term psychological support outside the university is recommended. You should leave the first appointment with a clear sense of next steps.

## ANU After-Hours Crisis Contacts and Emergency Support

Mental health crises do not always happen during business hours. The **ANU after-hours crisis contacts** system ensures that students have access to support 24 hours a day, seven days a week. If you are experiencing a mental health emergency outside of 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM on weekdays, or at any time on weekends and public holidays, several options are available.

The **ANU Crisis Support Line** can be reached at **1300 050 327** or by texting **0488 884 170**. This service is operated by a dedicated crisis support provider and is available to all ANU students. It offers immediate, confidential telephone counselling for urgent situations including suicidal thoughts, severe distress, or recent traumatic experiences. For on-campus emergencies where immediate physical safety is a concern, ANU Security can be contacted at **02 6125 2249** and can coordinate an emergency response, including ambulance attendance if necessary.

Additionally, students can access national services such as **Lifeline on 13 11 14** and the **Suicide Call Back Service on 1300 659 467**. These are not ANU-specific but provide 24-hour crisis support across Australia. It is worth saving these numbers in your phone before you need them.

## ANU Academic Adjustments for Mental Health Conditions

Mental health challenges can directly impact your ability to meet academic deadlines, attend classes, or perform in examinations. The **ANU academic adjustments mental health** pathway exists to ensure that students with mental health conditions are not disadvantaged in their studies. Academic adjustments, previously known as special consideration or reasonable adjustments, are formal accommodations made to your assessment or study requirements.

To access these adjustments, you typically need to register with **ANU Access and Inclusion**. This process involves providing documentation from a treating health professional, which can include your ANU counsellor or an external psychologist or GP. The documentation should outline the nature of your condition, its impact on your studies, and recommended adjustments. Common adjustments include **extensions on assignments, flexible exam arrangements such as extra time or a separate room, adjusted attendance requirements, and the ability to negotiate alternative assessment formats**.

Once registered, an Access and Inclusion advisor will work with you to develop an **Education Access Plan (EAP)**. This plan is shared only with the academic staff who need to implement the adjustments, and it does not disclose your specific diagnosis. The EAP is a living document that can be reviewed and updated as your circumstances change. It is important to initiate this process early rather than waiting until a crisis point in the semester.

## How to Prepare for Your First Appointment

Arriving prepared can reduce anxiety and help you make the most of your initial session. Consider writing down a few bullet points about what you want to discuss. This might include **specific symptoms you have noticed**, such as changes in sleep, appetite, concentration, or mood; any significant recent events or stressors; and what you hope counselling will help you achieve.

It is also helpful to bring your **student ID card** and any relevant medical information, including a list of current medications if applicable. If you have previously accessed mental health support elsewhere, noting down those details can assist your clinician in understanding what has or has not worked for you in the past. Finally, plan to arrive a few minutes early to complete any brief paperwork and settle into the space. The Kambri Health and Wellbeing Centre has a quiet waiting area where you can sit before your appointment begins.

## Ongoing Support Options Beyond Individual Counselling

Individual counselling is just one component of the **ANU mental health support process**. The university offers a range of complementary services designed to build coping skills and foster connection. **Group programs** run throughout the semester on topics such as managing anxiety, mindfulness, and building resilience. These groups typically meet weekly for four to six sessions and are facilitated by experienced clinicians.

ANU also partners with **Sonder**, a 24/7 safety and wellbeing app that provides chat-based support and check-in services. Through Sonder, you can access immediate text-based support for non-crisis concerns and receive practical assistance with wellbeing management. Additionally, the university offers **self-help resources** through the Counselling and Wellbeing website, including guided modules on procrastination, perfectionism, and sleep improvement. These resources are available at any time and can be used independently or alongside counselling.

## FAQ

**How long will I wait for my first ANU counselling appointment in 2026?**
Waiting times vary depending on the time of semester and urgency of your situation. For non-urgent appointments, the typical wait is between **7 and 14 days**. If you are assessed as needing more immediate support during the triage call, you may be offered an appointment within **24 to 48 hours**. During peak periods such as examination weeks, the Centre increases capacity to reduce wait times.

**Can I request a specific counsellor at ANU?**
Yes, you can express a preference for a counsellor of a particular gender or with experience in certain areas, such as cross-cultural counselling or trauma-informed care. The intake team will accommodate these requests where possible, though availability may mean a longer wait. If you have previously seen a specific clinician and wish to continue with them, mention this during the booking call.

**What if I need more than short-term counselling?**
The ANU service is designed for short to medium-term support, typically up to **six sessions per year**. If your clinician determines that you would benefit from longer-term therapy, they will assist you with a referral to a community-based psychologist or help you access a **Mental Health Care Plan** through a GP, which provides Medicare rebates for up to **10 individual sessions per calendar year** in 2026.

**Does ANU counselling provide documentation for academic adjustments?**
Yes, ANU counsellors can provide supporting documentation for Access and Inclusion registration. This documentation will outline the functional impact of your mental health condition without necessarily disclosing a diagnosis. It is recommended that you discuss this need with your counsellor early in your sessions so they can prepare the appropriate paperwork.
