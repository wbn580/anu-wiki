Navigating the Australian job market as an international student requires a clear understanding of your legal entitlements, tax obligations, and the practical realities of securing employment. At the Australian National University, over **6,800 international students** from more than 100 countries are currently enrolled in 2026, many of whom rely on part-time work to supplement their living expenses in Canberra. According to the Department of Home Affairs, international students in Australia contributed over **$25.5 billion** to the economy in the 2025 calendar year, with part-time employment forming a significant portion of this engagement.

This guide breaks down everything ANU international students need to know about work hours, taxation, and job hunting in 2026, drawing on the latest visa regulations and university-specific resources. Whether you are a newly arrived undergraduate or a research candidate planning your finances, the information below will help you work legally, manage your tax efficiently, and find suitable roles that complement your academic schedule.

## Understanding the 48-Hour Fortnightly Work Cap

**Student visa work rights** are defined by the conditions attached to your Subclass 500 visa. As of 2026, the standard restriction permits international students to work **up to 48 hours per fortnight** during teaching periods. A fortnight is calculated as any 14-day period starting on a Monday, meaning your hours reset every second week. This cap applies to all types of paid employment, including casual, part-time, and contract roles, whether you are working for a single employer or multiple organisations.

The 48-hour limit is strictly enforced by the Department of Home Affairs, and breaches can result in visa cancellation. However, there are important exceptions to be aware of. **Unlimited work hours** are permitted during scheduled university breaks, such as the mid-year winter recess in July and the summer holiday period from late November through to late February. If your course includes a mandatory work placement or an internship that is a registered component of your degree, those hours do not count toward the 48-hour cap, provided the placement is formally assessed and credited.

**PhD and Masters by research students** should note that while their primary activity is research, the same work restrictions apply unless they have completed all coursework components and are in a full-time thesis stage where no formal teaching periods are scheduled. It is strongly recommended that you check your individual Confirmation of Enrolment to confirm the exact dates of your teaching periods, as these define when the cap is active.

## Tax File Number Registration and Australian Tax Basics

Before you can legally receive a salary in Australia, you must obtain a **Tax File Number**, commonly abbreviated as TFN. This unique nine-digit identifier is issued by the Australian Taxation Office and is essential for ensuring you are taxed at the correct rate. Without a TFN, your employer is legally required to withhold tax at the highest marginal rate of **47%**, which can drastically reduce your take-home pay. The application process is free and can be completed online through the ATO website once you have arrived in Australia and have a valid visa and residential address.

In 2026, the **tax-free threshold** for Australian residents for tax purposes remains at **$18,200**. Most international students who are enrolled in a course lasting more than six months and who reside in Australia for the majority of the financial year are considered residents for tax purposes and are entitled to this threshold. This means you will not pay any income tax on the first $18,200 you earn each financial year, which runs from July 1 to June 30.

It is critical to understand the distinction between an employee and a contractor. Most part-time jobs on campus and in retail or hospitality will classify you as an employee, meaning your employer withholds tax from each pay and contributes to your superannuation. **Superannuation** is a mandatory retirement savings scheme where your employer contributes an additional **11.5%** of your ordinary time earnings into a designated super fund. When you permanently depart Australia, you may be eligible to claim this accumulated superannuation back through a Departing Australia Superannuation Payment, though this is taxed at a concessional rate.

## On-Campus Employment Opportunities at ANU

**ANU campus jobs** are among the most convenient and student-friendly employment options available. The university operates a centralised casual employment register known as the **ANU Student Casual Professional Register**, which allows current students to express interest in administrative, customer service, research assistant, and event support roles across various colleges and service divisions. These positions are designed with student schedules in mind, often offering shifts that fit between lectures and tutorials.

The **ANU Students’ Association** and the **Postgraduate and Research Students’ Association** also hire student representatives, peer mentors, and support staff throughout the academic year. These roles not only provide income but also build valuable professional networks within the university community. Additionally, the university library system, the ANU Sport and Recreation Association, and residential halls frequently recruit students for reception, maintenance, and tutoring positions.

Pay rates for on-campus roles are governed by the **ANU Enterprise Agreement**, which generally exceeds the national minimum wage. As of 2026, casual student employees at ANU typically earn between **$32.50 and $41.80 per hour**, depending on the classification and required skills. These roles often come with the added benefit of a supportive work environment that understands your academic commitments and exam periods, making them a strong first choice for international students navigating the Australian workplace for the first time.

## Job Hunting Strategies in the Canberra Market

Canberra’s labour market is distinct from larger Australian cities, with a high concentration of public sector and service industry roles. For international students, the most accessible sectors are **hospitality, retail, and administrative support**. The ANU campus itself is located within walking distance of the Canberra Centre, Civic, and Braddon precincts, which host numerous cafes, restaurants, and retail outlets that regularly hire casual staff. The **ANU Careers and Employability** service offers a dedicated online job board called CareerHub, where local employers specifically target ANU students.

When preparing your application materials, tailor your resume to Australian conventions. A standard Australian resume is typically **two to three pages** and includes a professional summary, education details, relevant work experience with bullet-point accomplishments, and at least two local referees. Avoid including a photograph, marital status, or date of birth, as these are not standard practice and can introduce unconscious bias. The Careers team runs weekly workshops on resume writing and interview skills, which are free for all enrolled students.

Networking remains a powerful tool in Canberra’s relatively compact professional community. Attend university career fairs, guest lectures, and industry networking events advertised through the ANU events calendar. LinkedIn is widely used by Australian employers, and maintaining an up-to-date profile with a clear indication of your work rights can help you connect with recruiters. When reaching out directly to businesses, a polite in-person visit with a printed resume during quiet trading hours is often more effective than online applications for casual hospitality roles.

## Managing Your Payslip, Superannuation, and Tax Returns

Every time you are paid, your employer must provide you with a **payslip** within one working day. This document must detail your gross pay, the amount of tax withheld, any superannuation contributions, and your hourly rate. Review each payslip carefully to ensure your TFN has been recorded correctly and that you are not being overtaxed. If you notice discrepancies, raise them with your employer immediately and keep all payslips for the duration of your stay, as they are essential for your end-of-year tax return.

The Australian financial year ends on **June 30**, and you are required to lodge a tax return by **October 31** if you have earned any income during that period. The ATO’s online myTax system is straightforward for students with simple financial affairs, and the **ANU Tax Clinic**, run by the College of Law, offers free assistance to students and low-income earners who need help preparing their returns. This clinic operates from July to October each year and is an excellent resource for international students unfamiliar with the Australian tax system.

When you finish your studies and leave Australia, you can claim your superannuation back through a Departing Australia Superannuation Payment (DASP). This can be applied for online through the ATO, and you will need to provide proof that your visa has expired or been cancelled. The payment is taxed at **35% for the taxed element** of your super, though this rate is lower than the standard marginal rate for non-residents. It is wise to keep your super fund contact details current even after departing, as the process can take several months.

## Balancing Work and Study: Practical Considerations

While part-time work provides financial relief and valuable experience, overcommitting can jeopardise your academic performance and visa compliance. ANU’s academic calendar is intensive, with a standard full-time load of **four courses per semester**, each requiring approximately **10 to 12 hours of independent study** per week in addition to contact hours. If you are working close to the 48-hour fortnightly cap during teaching periods, you are effectively managing a full-time work and study schedule, which is unsustainable for most students.

Plan your work commitments around your assessment schedule. ANU publishes key dates for examination periods and assignment deadlines at the start of each semester, and you should block out these weeks for reduced work hours well in advance. Communicate openly with your employer about your academic obligations; many Canberra businesses that hire students are accustomed to flexible rostering around exam periods. If you are struggling, ANU offers free counselling services and academic skills workshops through the **Student Wellbeing and Learning Centre**, which can help you develop time management strategies.

Remember that your primary purpose in Australia is to complete your qualification successfully. The work rights attached to your visa are a privilege designed to supplement your experience, not a guarantee of full-time employment. Treat part-time work as one component of a broader strategy that includes networking, internships, and academic achievement, all of which contribute to your long-term career prospects after graduation.

## FAQ

**How many hours can I work on a student visa at ANU during semester breaks in 2026?**
During scheduled university breaks, such as the winter recess in July and the summer holiday period from late November to late February, you can work unlimited hours. This is confirmed by the Department of Home Affairs under Subclass 500 visa conditions for 2026. You must confirm the exact break dates with your ANU academic calendar, as teaching periods vary by program.

**What tax rate will I pay if I earn less than $18,200 in the 2026 financial year?**
If you are classified as an Australian resident for tax purposes and earn less than $18,200 between July 1, 2025, and June 30, 2026, you will pay zero income tax due to the tax-free threshold. You must still lodge a tax return to confirm this, and if your employer has withheld any tax, it will be refunded to you after your return is processed.

**Can I work more than 48 hours a fortnight if my ANU course includes a mandatory internship?**
Yes. If the internship is a formal, credit-bearing component of your enrolled course and is registered as part of your Confirmation of Enrolment, the hours spent on that placement do not count toward your 48-hour fortnightly cap. This exception only applies to placements that are essential to completing your qualification, not voluntary or extracurricular work experience.
