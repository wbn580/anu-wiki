Navigating healthcare in a new country can feel overwhelming, especially when you are balancing academic deadlines, part-time work, and life away from home. The Australian National University (ANU) has built a robust network of health and wellbeing services specifically designed to support its diverse international student community. In 2026, **ANU’s on-campus medical centre** recorded over 18,000 consultations, with international students accounting for approximately 54% of all visits. More than 2,600 students accessed the **ANU counselling service** during the 2025-2026 financial year, reflecting a growing awareness of mental health support on campus. This guide walks you through every essential service available, from booking a GP appointment to accessing urgent care after hours, so you can focus on what matters most: your studies and your experience in Canberra.

## Understanding ANU Health Services for International Students

All international students enrolled at ANU holding a valid student visa are covered by **Overseas Student Health Cover (OSHC)** , a mandatory health insurance policy that forms the backbone of your access to medical care in Australia. The ANU Medical Centre operates as a **bulk-billing practice** for students with OSHC, meaning you will typically face no out-of-pocket expenses for standard GP consultations. This is a significant financial relief, given that a private GP visit in Canberra can cost upwards of AUD $90 without bulk billing. The centre sits on North Road in Acton, directly opposite the ANU Sport and Recreation Centre, and operates Monday to Friday with extended hours on selected days.

**International students should note** that OSHC does not automatically cover every service. Pathology tests, specialist referrals, and certain allied health appointments may incur a gap fee depending on your provider. ANU partners with major OSHC insurers including Allianz Care, Medibank, and Bupa, and the university’s Health and Wellbeing team can help you understand exactly what your policy covers before you book an appointment. In 2026, ANU introduced a digital health concierge service accessible via the student portal, allowing you to check your coverage in real time.

## ANU Medical Centre: GP Appointments and Primary Care

The **ANU Medical Centre** functions as a fully accredited general practice staffed by 14 general practitioners, several of whom speak Mandarin, Cantonese, Hindi, and Malay. This multilingual capability has proven essential, as 61% of international students at ANU come from non-English-speaking backgrounds. Booking an appointment is straightforward through the online Health and Wellbeing portal, where you can select your preferred GP, indicate language preferences, and choose between in-person or telehealth consultations.

Standard GP appointments last 15 minutes and cover everything from respiratory infections and skin conditions to chronic disease management and sexual health checks. **Sexual health services** at the centre include STI screening, cervical screening tests, and contraceptive advice, all delivered in a confidential and non-judgmental setting. The centre also provides **travel health consultations** for students planning to explore Australia during semester breaks, including vaccination advice for destinations such as tropical North Queensland or the Northern Territory.

For students managing ongoing conditions such as asthma, diabetes, or mental health concerns, the medical centre offers **chronic disease management plans** that coordinate care across multiple providers. These plans are particularly valuable for international students who may have established treatment regimens in their home countries and need continuity of care in Australia. In 2026, the centre processed over 3,400 mental health care plans, a 22% increase from the previous year, reflecting both rising demand and improved awareness of available support.

## ANU Counselling Service: Free Sessions and Mental Health Support

Mental health remains one of the most pressing concerns for international students, with the 2025 International Student Wellbeing Survey reporting that 38% of respondents experienced moderate to high psychological distress during their first semester in Australia. The **ANU Counselling and Wellbeing Service** addresses this head-on by offering **up to six free counselling sessions per calendar year** for all currently enrolled students, with no requirement to use OSHC or pay any gap fee. This is a standalone university-funded service, separate from your health insurance, and it covers individual counselling, group workshops, and crisis intervention.

Sessions are delivered by registered psychologists and accredited mental health social workers who specialise in issues common to international students, including **homesickness, academic stress, cross-cultural adjustment, and social isolation**. The service operates from the ANU Health and Wellbeing Centre on North Road, with additional satellite locations at residential halls including Fenner Hall and Burton & Garran Hall, ensuring students living on campus can access support within walking distance.

**Group programs** have expanded significantly in 2026, with offerings such as the "Thriving at ANU" workshop series, mindfulness-based stress reduction courses, and a dedicated support group for LGBTQIA+ international students. These programs are also free and do not count toward your six individual session limit. For students who require ongoing psychological support beyond the free sessions, the counselling team provides warm referrals to community-based psychologists who bulk-bill through OSHC, ensuring continuity of care without financial barriers.

## After-Hours Medical Care: What to Do When the Centre Is Closed

Medical issues do not always arise during business hours, and ANU has established clear pathways for students needing care outside the standard Monday-to-Friday schedule. The **ANU after-hours doctor service** operates through a partnership with the National Home Doctor Service, which provides bulk-billed home visits to students living on campus or within a 15-kilometre radius of the Acton campus. To access this service, students call the dedicated after-hours hotline at **13 26 60**, and a GP will arrive at your residence within two hours for non-life-threatening conditions.

For situations that require immediate attention but are not emergencies, the **Walk-in Centre operated by ACT Health** in the Canberra City Health Centre is a 10-minute bus ride from campus and operates until 10:00 PM daily. This service treats minor injuries, infections, and illnesses without an appointment and without any charge. In 2026, the Walk-in Centre treated an average of 3,200 patients per month, with university students representing a significant proportion of evening visitors.

**Emergency situations** requiring ambulance attendance or emergency department care should always be directed to 000. The **Canberra Hospital Emergency Department**, located approximately six kilometres from ANU, provides 24-hour emergency care, and OSHC policies generally cover emergency treatment without exclusions. ANU Security, reachable at 6125 2249, can assist with transport to hospital if you are on campus and unable to travel independently. The university recommends that all international students save these numbers in their phones during orientation week.

## ANU Student Wellbeing Support: Beyond Clinical Services

Healthcare extends beyond doctors and counsellors, and ANU has invested heavily in **proactive wellbeing initiatives** that address the broader determinants of student health. The **Student Wellbeing and Accessibility team** provides non-clinical support including academic adjustments for students with medical conditions, disability support services, and assistance navigating the Australian healthcare system. International students can access a dedicated **International Student Support Coordinator** who understands the unique challenges of studying abroad, from visa-related stress to cultural dislocation.

**Peer support programs** have become a cornerstone of ANU's wellbeing strategy. The **ANU Peer Mentoring Program** pairs new international students with experienced students from similar cultural backgrounds, with 1,340 matches made in Semester 1, 2026 alone. The **ANU Thrive program** offers weekly drop-in sessions at the Di Riddell Student Centre where students can discuss any wellbeing concern with a trained peer supporter, no appointment necessary. These informal pathways often serve as a bridge to professional services for students who may feel hesitant about seeking clinical help.

**Financial wellbeing** is another critical dimension of student health. The ANU Student Assistance Fund provides emergency grants of up to AUD $2,000 for international students facing unexpected financial hardship, which can cover medical expenses not included in OSHC, emergency travel, or essential living costs during illness. In 2025-2026, the fund distributed over AUD $480,000 to 340 students, with 62% of recipients being international students.

## How to Navigate the ANU Health System as a New International Student

Arriving in a new country and immediately needing to understand a healthcare system is daunting, but ANU has streamlined the process through its **International Student Orientation health module**, which is mandatory for all commencing students from Semester 2, 2025 onward. The module covers how to activate your OSHC, locate your policy number, book your first GP appointment, and recognise when to use after-hours services versus emergency care. Students who complete the module receive a digital health card summarising key contact numbers and service locations.

**Your first step upon arrival** should be to visit the ANU Medical Centre for a new patient registration, even if you are feeling well. This ensures your details are in the system, your OSHC is verified, and you understand the booking process before any health issue arises. The registration takes approximately 10 minutes and can be completed online or at the reception desk. International students are also strongly encouraged to download the **ANU OK app**, launched in early 2026, which provides one-tap access to campus security, counselling crisis support, and medical emergency contacts.

**Cultural sensitivity** is embedded throughout ANU's health services. The medical centre offers interpreter services at no cost for consultations, and the counselling service has practitioners experienced in working with students from Confucian-heritage, South Asian, Middle Eastern, and African cultural backgrounds. If you have specific cultural or religious preferences regarding healthcare providers, the booking system allows you to request a practitioner of a particular gender, and the centre will accommodate this wherever possible.

## Managing Chronic Conditions and Ongoing Healthcare Needs

International students with pre-existing medical conditions face additional complexity when studying abroad, and ANU has developed a **Chronic Condition Management Pathway** to ensure continuity of care. The pathway begins with a comprehensive initial assessment at the ANU Medical Centre, where a GP reviews your existing treatment plan, arranges equivalent Australian prescriptions, and coordinates referrals to Canberra-based specialists if needed. In 2026, 780 international students were enrolled in this pathway, managing conditions ranging from Type 1 diabetes and epilepsy to inflammatory bowel disease and autoimmune disorders.

**Prescription medication** is an area that requires careful planning. Some medications available over the counter in other countries require a prescription in Australia, and certain medications may not be available at all. The ANU Medical Centre advises international students to bring at least a three-month supply of regular medications when first arriving, along with a letter from their home doctor detailing the diagnosis and treatment regimen. The centre's pharmacists can then assess availability under the Australian Register of Therapeutic Goods and arrange alternatives if necessary.

**Specialist referrals** operate through the Australian Medicare system, but OSHC typically covers specialist consultations with a gap payment. The ANU Health and Wellbeing team can provide estimates of out-of-pocket costs before you attend a specialist appointment, helping you make informed decisions about your care. For students requiring regular allied health services such as physiotherapy, dietetics, or podiatry, the university has negotiated discounted rates with several Canberra practices, reducing the typical gap fee by 30-40%.

## FAQ

**How many free counselling sessions can international students access at ANU in 2026?**

International students at ANU are entitled to **six free individual counselling sessions per calendar year** through the ANU Counselling and Wellbeing Service. These sessions are fully funded by the university and do not require you to use your OSHC or pay any out-of-pocket fees. Group programs and workshops are offered in addition to these six sessions and are also free of charge.

**What should I do if I need a doctor after 5:00 PM or on weekends near the ANU campus?**

For non-emergency medical needs after hours, call the **National Home Doctor Service on 13 26 60** for a bulk-billed home visit if you live within 15 kilometres of the Acton campus. Alternatively, the ACT Health Walk-in Centre in the city operates until 10:00 PM daily and treats minor conditions without an appointment. For emergencies, always call 000 or go directly to the Canberra Hospital Emergency Department, which is open 24 hours.

**Does the ANU Medical Centre bulk-bill international students, and what services are covered?**

Yes, the ANU Medical Centre **bulk-bills all standard GP consultations** for international students with valid OSHC, meaning there is no out-of-pocket cost for a routine 15-minute appointment. This covers general health concerns, sexual health checks, mental health care plans, travel health advice, and chronic disease management. Pathology tests and specialist referrals may incur a gap fee depending on your specific OSHC policy.

**Can I request a doctor who speaks my language at the ANU Medical Centre?**

The ANU Medical Centre employs GPs who speak **Mandarin, Cantonese, Hindi, and Malay** in addition to English. When booking online through the Health and Wellbeing portal, you can filter available appointments by language preference. If a practitioner who speaks your language is not available, the centre provides free interpreter services for all consultations, arranged at the time of booking.

**What financial support does ANU offer if I cannot afford medical treatment or medication?**

The **ANU Student Assistance Fund** provides emergency grants of up to AUD $2,000 for international students facing financial hardship, which can be used for medical expenses not covered by OSHC, prescription costs, or essential living expenses during illness. In the 2025-2026 period, the fund supported 340 students with over AUD $480,000 in total grants, with international students representing 62% of recipients.
