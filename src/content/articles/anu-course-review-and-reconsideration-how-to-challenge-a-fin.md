Receiving an unexpected final grade or facing an adverse academic decision can be deeply unsettling. At the Australian National University, approximately **12% of students** will request some form of assessment review during their studies, and nearly **7% of formal appeals** result in a grade modification according to internal ANU data from early 2026. Understanding the structured pathways available to you is not just about potentially changing an outcome—it is about exercising your fundamental rights as a student within a transparent academic governance framework.

This guide walks you through every stage of the **ANU grade appeal** and **ANU academic reconsideration** process, from informal clarification requests through to formal university-level complaints. We will cover strict timelines, required documentation, common pitfalls, and strategic considerations that can make the difference between a dismissed application and a successful resolution.

## Understanding ANU Academic Decisions and Your Right to Challenge

Not every unfavourable outcome qualifies for formal appeal. The university distinguishes between **academic decisions**—which include final course grades, assessment marks, progression determinations, and academic integrity findings—and procedural or administrative matters. Under the ANU Assessment Rule 2026, students have the right to seek review of any academic decision that directly affects their enrolment status or academic record.

It is critical to recognise that **disagreement with a mark alone** is insufficient grounds for a successful appeal. The ANU academic reconsideration process requires you to demonstrate specific grounds: procedural irregularity, factual error in assessment, inconsistent application of published criteria, or failure to consider relevant evidence. Simply believing you deserved a higher grade will not carry your case forward. The university's Academic Appeals Committee reviewed **over 340 cases** in the 2025 academic year, with only those presenting clear documentary evidence of error or inconsistency proceeding to full hearing.

Before initiating any formal challenge, you should obtain a clear understanding of the decision itself. Request a **detailed marking breakdown** from your course convener, review the course outline and assessment rubrics, and compare your work against the published grade descriptors. This preliminary step often resolves concerns without escalation and provides the factual foundation necessary for any subsequent **ANU complaint process**.

## Step 1: Informal Resolution with Course Conveners

The Assessment Rule mandates that students must first attempt informal resolution before lodging any formal **ANU assessment review**. This is not merely a procedural formality—approximately **60% of grade concerns** are resolved at this stage without further escalation, according to 2026 data from the ANU Dean of Students office.

You must contact your course convener within **10 working days** of the official release of results. The clock starts from the date results are published on ISIS, not from when you first view them. Your email should be professional, specific, and evidence-based. State clearly which assessment item or decision you are querying, reference the published marking criteria, and identify precisely where you believe an error occurred. Attach any supporting documentation, such as marked rubrics, feedback comments, or relevant correspondence.

**Do not** make emotional appeals or reference your overall GPA, scholarship requirements, or future career plans. These factors are immaterial to the academic merits of your case and can undermine your credibility. Instead, frame your request as seeking clarification: "I would appreciate guidance on how my response to criterion 3 was assessed against the HD descriptor, as my submission addressed all three required elements as outlined in the rubric." This approach invites dialogue rather than defensiveness.

If the convener agrees that an error occurred, they can initiate a **grade amendment** directly. If they maintain the original decision, they must provide you with written reasons. This documentation becomes essential if you proceed to formal reconsideration, as it demonstrates that you have completed the required informal stage.

## Step 2: Formal ANU Academic Reconsideration Application

When informal resolution fails or the convener does not respond within **5 working days**, you may escalate to a formal **ANU academic reconsideration**. This application is submitted through the ANU Student Complaints and Appeals Portal and must be lodged within **20 working days** of the original decision or the conclusion of informal discussions, whichever is later.

The formal application requires several specific components. You must submit a **written statement** that identifies the decision being challenged, specifies the grounds for reconsideration with reference to the Assessment Rule, and provides a chronological account of your informal resolution attempts. Attach all relevant evidence: the course outline, assessment instructions, your submitted work, marking rubrics, feedback received, email correspondence with the convener, and any expert opinion you have obtained.

The **Associate Dean (Education)** of your college will review your application and may consult with the original assessor, an independent academic, or both. Under the 2026 procedures, the Associate Dean must complete their review and issue a written determination within **15 working days**. Possible outcomes include upholding the original decision, amending the grade, ordering a re-mark by an independent examiner, or granting a supplementary assessment opportunity.

Be aware that a **re-mark can result in a lower grade**. The reconsideration process is a complete review of the academic merits, not a one-way upward adjustment. Before submitting your application, honestly evaluate whether your work could withstand independent scrutiny against the published standards. In 2025, approximately **4% of reconsideration cases** resulted in a lower final grade than the original.

## Grounds for Appeal and Evidence Requirements

The success of any **ANU academic decision challenge** hinges on the strength and specificity of your grounds. The Assessment Rule recognises three primary categories: **procedural irregularity**, **factual error**, and **manifest unreasonableness**. Each requires different types of evidence and carries distinct burdens of proof.

Procedural irregularity occurs when the university fails to follow its own published policies. Examples include an assessor not holding required qualifications, failure to provide reasonable adjustment for a registered disability, or marking against criteria not disclosed in the course outline. To succeed on this ground, you must identify the specific procedure breached and provide evidence of the breach. A **disability access plan** registered with Access and Inclusion, combined with correspondence showing the adjustment was not implemented, would constitute strong evidence.

Factual error involves an objective mistake in the assessment process. This might include a mathematical error in calculating marks, omission of an entire section from assessment, or reliance on incorrect information. You must demonstrate that the error is **material**—meaning it could have affected the outcome—not merely incidental. A spreadsheet showing incorrect formula application or an assessor's acknowledgment of oversight would support this ground.

Manifest unreasonableness is the highest threshold. You must show that no reasonable academic, applying the published criteria to your work, could have reached the same conclusion. This requires comparative evidence, such as exemplars of work receiving higher grades that are demonstrably equivalent to yours, or expert testimony from a qualified academic in the same discipline. The **ANU Student Association** offers free advocacy services that can help you assess whether your case meets this standard before you invest time and emotional energy in a formal challenge.

## The ANU Complaint Process: Beyond Individual Grades

Some academic concerns extend beyond a single assessment decision and require engagement with the broader **ANU complaint process**. This pathway is appropriate when you have concerns about systemic issues: course design or delivery, supervision quality in research degrees, discriminatory treatment, or failure to provide promised learning resources.

The complaint process is managed by the **University Ombudsman**, an independent office established to handle student grievances that cannot be resolved at the local level. Unlike the reconsideration pathway, which focuses narrowly on the academic merits of a specific decision, the complaint process can examine conduct, policy compliance, and institutional practices. In 2026, the Ombudsman received **over 580 formal complaints**, with the most common categories being assessment feedback quality, inconsistent marking across tutorial groups, and communication failures.

Filing a complaint does not automatically pause other processes. If you have a pending reconsideration application, you should still meet those deadlines. The Ombudsman can make recommendations including **apologies, remedial action, policy review, or compensation** for direct loss. However, the Ombudsman cannot directly change a grade—that authority remains with the academic decision-makers under the Assessment Rule. The complaint process is complementary to, not a substitute for, formal grade appeal mechanisms.

Documentation is equally important here. Maintain a **chronological log** of all interactions, save every email and written communication, and note the names and positions of staff members you speak with. If your complaint involves multiple courses or systemic issues, consider preparing a summary table that maps each concern to specific policy provisions, dates, and evidence. This organisational work significantly strengthens your case.

## External Review: ANU Appeals and the ACT Ombudsman

If you have exhausted all internal **ANU grade appeal** and complaint mechanisms and remain dissatisfied, external review options are available. The highest internal level is the **ANU Appeals Committee**, which reviews procedural fairness rather than academic merits. You must apply within **15 working days** of receiving the final internal determination, and you must demonstrate that the reconsideration or complaint process itself was flawed—not merely that you disagree with the outcome.

The Appeals Committee can identify procedural defects and remit matters for fresh consideration, but it cannot substitute its own academic judgment. In the 2025 academic year, the Committee received **127 applications** and found procedural error warranting remittal in **31 cases**. Common successful grounds include failure to provide adequate reasons, consideration of irrelevant material, or bias in the original decision-making process.

Beyond the university, students may approach the **ACT Ombudsman** under the Ombudsman Act 1989 (ACT). The Ombudsman can investigate complaints about administrative actions of the university, including unreasonable delay, failure to follow procedures, or conduct that is oppressive or improperly discriminatory. The Ombudsman's powers are recommendatory, not binding, but its findings carry significant weight. In practice, ANU has implemented Ombudsman recommendations in **over 90% of cases** since 2024.

International students should also be aware that unresolved academic complaints may have visa implications. The **Department of Home Affairs** expects students to maintain satisfactory course progress, and a pending appeal may affect your enrolment status. Seek advice from the ANU International Student Support team before initiating processes that could delay your progression.

## Strategic Considerations and Common Mistakes

Navigating the **ANU academic reconsideration** process requires more than understanding procedures—it demands strategic judgment. The most common mistake students make is **acting too quickly without adequate preparation**. Once you submit a formal application, the grounds are fixed. You cannot later introduce new arguments or evidence unless exceptional circumstances arise. Take the full 20 working days if needed to gather documentation and refine your written statement.

Another frequent error is **conflating procedural and merits review**. If your real concern is that the convener was rude or unhelpful, that is a conduct complaint, not a grade appeal. Filing under the wrong pathway will result in dismissal and may prejudice subsequent attempts to use the correct process. The ANU Student Advocacy Service can help you determine the appropriate mechanism before you commit to a course of action.

**Independent evidence** dramatically strengthens your case. If you believe your work was marked inconsistently with published criteria, obtain an opinion from another academic in the same field. This might be a tutor from a different tutorial group, a lecturer in a related course, or an academic at another institution. Their written assessment, mapping your work against the rubric, provides the comparative analysis that reconsideration officers find persuasive. Without such evidence, your application reduces to your word against the assessor's, and the assessor's professional judgment will prevail.

Finally, maintain **professional conduct throughout**. Aggressive correspondence, social media posts about your case, or attempts to pressure staff will harm your credibility and may constitute misconduct. The university takes a dim view of students who impugn academic integrity without foundation. Even if you feel frustrated, keep all communications measured, factual, and respectful. The strength of your case, not the volume of your complaint, determines outcomes.

## FAQ

### Q: How long do I have to submit an ANU grade appeal after results are released?
You have **10 working days** from the official results publication date to initiate informal discussions with your course convener. If informal resolution fails, you then have **20 working days** from the conclusion of those discussions to lodge a formal reconsideration application through the ANU Student Complaints and Appeals Portal. The total window from results release to formal application cannot exceed approximately **30 working days** in most cases, though extensions may be granted in exceptional circumstances with supporting documentation.

### Q: Can my grade go down if I request an ANU assessment review?
Yes. A formal reconsideration involves a complete re-evaluation of your work against published criteria, and the outcome can be **lower, higher, or unchanged**. In the 2025 academic year, approximately **4% of reconsideration cases** resulted in a lower final grade. Before submitting your application, carefully assess whether an independent examiner would likely confirm or reduce your original mark. If you are borderline between two grade bands, the risk of a downward adjustment may outweigh the potential benefit.

### Q: What is the difference between an ANU academic reconsideration and a complaint to the University Ombudsman?
An academic reconsideration focuses narrowly on the **merits of a specific assessment decision** and can result in a grade change if error is found. A complaint to the University Ombudsman addresses **conduct, process, or systemic issues** and can result in recommendations for remedial action, apologies, or policy changes, but the Ombudsman cannot directly alter a grade. You may pursue both pathways simultaneously if your concerns include both assessment accuracy and procedural or conduct matters. In 2026, approximately **15% of formal complaints** were lodged alongside active reconsideration applications.

### Q: What evidence do I need for a successful ANU academic decision challenge based on procedural irregularity?
You must provide **documentary proof** that the university failed to follow its own published policies. Strong evidence includes the specific policy provision allegedly breached, correspondence showing the breach occurred, and any supporting materials such as disability access plans, timetables, or witness statements. For example, if you were denied extra writing time approved in a **2026 Access and Inclusion plan**, you would submit the plan itself, the exam timetable showing standard time allocation, and any email confirming the denial. Procedural claims without contemporaneous documentation are rarely successful, as the university's records are presumed accurate.
