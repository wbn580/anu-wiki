International education in Australia continues to expand, with the **Australian National University (ANU)** hosting over 9,000 international students from more than 130 countries as of 2026. Maintaining **student visa compliance** is not merely a bureaucratic formality—it directly affects your ability to remain in Australia and complete your degree. The Department of Home Affairs recorded over 47,000 student visa cancellations nationwide in the 2025–2026 financial year, with non-compliance in course progress and attendance among the leading causes. This guide explains exactly what ANU monitors, the thresholds you must meet, and how the university fulfills its reporting obligations under the Education Services for Overseas Students (ESOS) framework.

## Understanding ANU Visa Compliance Fundamentals

ANU operates as a registered CRICOS provider (CRICOS code 00120C) and is legally required to monitor international students under the **National Code of Practice for Providers of Education and Training to Overseas Students**. Every international student holding a **Subclass 500 Student Visa** agrees to visa conditions that include maintaining satisfactory course progress, meeting attendance requirements where applicable, and keeping enrolment details current. ANU’s compliance framework aligns with the **Department of Home Affairs** expectations and the ESOS Act, meaning the university must report students who fail to meet these standards. The consequences of non-compliance range from formal intervention and counselling to mandatory reporting that can trigger visa cancellation proceedings. For students, understanding these mechanisms is the first step toward avoiding unintended breaches.

## Course Progress Monitoring at ANU

**Course progress monitoring** forms the backbone of ANU’s visa compliance system. The university assesses every international student’s academic performance at the end of each semester against the **satisfactory course progress** benchmark. Under the National Code, satisfactory progress requires completing at least 50% of enrolled units each semester and maintaining a pace that allows program completion within the expected duration as recorded on the Confirmation of Enrolment (CoE). ANU’s Division of Student Administration conducts systematic reviews using data from the Student Information System, flagging students who fall below the threshold. In 2026, ANU introduced enhanced progress analytics that identify at-risk students earlier, enabling proactive intervention before formal breach reporting becomes necessary.

### What Triggers an Unsatisfactory Progress Determination

Several patterns can trigger an **unsatisfactory course progress** determination. Failing two or more courses in a single semester is the most common trigger, but the system also catches students who withdraw after census dates without extenuating circumstances, those who repeatedly enrol in reduced course loads without approval, and students whose cumulative fails place them more than one semester behind their cohort. ANU applies a **two-semester review cycle**, meaning a student who underperforms in one semester receives a warning and must demonstrate improvement in the following term. Failure to return to satisfactory standing within that period results in mandatory reporting to the Department of Home Affairs. The university considers **special circumstances** such as serious illness, bereavement, or trauma, but these must be documented and submitted through formal channels before the review deadline.

## Attendance Thresholds for International Students

Attendance monitoring at ANU applies primarily to students enrolled in **foundation studies, English language programs, and certain diploma pathways** where structured face-to-face delivery is a core component. For degree-level coursework students, attendance is generally not tracked as a standalone visa compliance metric; instead, it factors indirectly through course progress and participation requirements. However, the **ANU College pathway programs** enforce a strict 80% attendance threshold per study period. Students who fall below this threshold without approved leave face intervention processes that escalate from counselling to formal breach notification. In 2026, ANU migrated attendance tracking to a digital check-in system that captures real-time data, reducing disputes and enabling faster identification of students at risk of breaching the 80% minimum.

### How Attendance Data Is Collected and Reviewed

ANU uses a combination of **electronic class registration, learning management system engagement logs, and lecturer-confirmed participation records** to compile attendance data. For programs subject to mandatory attendance monitoring, the system generates weekly reports that program coordinators review. A student whose attendance dips below 85% typically receives an early alert email, while the 80% threshold triggers a formal warning letter. The university retains discretion to approve **medical or compassionate leave** that exempts absences from the calculation, provided students submit supporting documentation within five business days of returning to class. International students should note that even in programs without explicit attendance thresholds, prolonged non-attendance that leads to course failure will surface through the course progress monitoring channel.

## Reporting Obligations to the Department of Home Affairs

ANU’s **reporting obligations** under the ESOS framework are non-negotiable. When a student breaches visa conditions related to course progress or attendance and has exhausted the university’s internal intervention and appeals processes, ANU must report the breach through the **Provider Registration and International Student Management System (PRISMS)**. Once a report is lodged, the Department of Home Affairs assesses whether to issue a **Notice of Intention to Consider Cancellation (NOICC)** of the student visa. ANU takes reporting seriously; in the 2025 academic year, the university submitted approximately 210 PRISMS reports for unsatisfactory progress and an additional 45 for attendance breaches across its pathway programs. These figures underscore the importance of addressing compliance issues at the earliest warning stage.

### The Intervention and Appeals Timeline

Before ANU can report a breach, it must follow a prescribed **intervention and appeals timeline**. The process begins with an early warning notification, followed by a formal notice of intention to report if the student does not return to compliance within the specified timeframe. Students have **20 working days** to access ANU’s internal complaints and appeals process, during which the university cannot lodge a PRISMS report. Appeals must demonstrate that the breach resulted from circumstances beyond the student’s control or that the university failed to apply its policies fairly. If an appeal is upheld, the reporting process stops. If denied or if the student does not appeal, ANU reports the breach within five business days of the deadline expiring. This timeline is strictly enforced, and students who miss appeal deadlines lose their opportunity to contest the finding at the institutional level.

## Maintaining Your Visa Status: Practical Strategies

Staying compliant requires proactive engagement with both academic and administrative systems. **Enrolment management** is the foundation—international students must remain enrolled full-time in every compulsory study period unless they have approved reduced study load or leave of absence. ANU allows international students to apply for a **reduced study load** only on compassionate or compelling grounds, and approval is not automatic. Common qualifying circumstances include a diagnosed medical condition that limits study capacity, a traumatic event, or a course structure issue beyond the student’s control. Requests must be submitted before the semester census date and supported by independent documentation. Taking an unauthorised reduced load places students at immediate compliance risk.

### Documentation and Record-Keeping

Effective **documentation and record-keeping** can make the difference between a resolved compliance concern and a reported breach. Students should retain all medical certificates, counsellor letters, legal documents, and correspondence with ANU staff related to their academic performance or attendance. When special circumstances arise, notify the university promptly—retrospective explanations carry significantly less weight than contemporaneous records. ANU’s **Student Central** and the **ANU Student Association** offer confidential advice and support for students navigating compliance processes. International students are also strongly encouraged to keep their contact details updated in the university system, as missing a warning email or letter does not constitute grounds for appeal.

## Common Misconceptions About ANU Visa Compliance

Several misconceptions circulate among international students that can lead to inadvertent breaches. One persistent belief is that **paying tuition on time** equates to visa compliance—it does not. Financial standing is a separate visa condition, and meeting it does not offset academic or attendance failures. Another misconception is that a **medical certificate automatically excuses all absences** or failed courses. While medical evidence is important, ANU assesses whether the condition genuinely prevented the student from meeting their obligations, and prolonged or recurrent issues may require a reduced study load or leave application rather than piecemeal certificates. Finally, some students assume that **changing to a lower-cost program** within ANU avoids scrutiny; in reality, any change that extends the CoE duration or alters the course level requires a new visa assessment and must be reported through PRISMS.

## FAQ

### What is the minimum course completion rate required to maintain an ANU student visa?
International students must complete at least **50% of enrolled units each semester** and maintain a pace that allows program completion within the CoE duration. Falling below this threshold for two consecutive semesters typically triggers a mandatory report to the Department of Home Affairs through PRISMS.

### How many days of absence trigger attendance reporting at ANU?
For programs subject to attendance monitoring, such as **ANU College pathway courses**, the threshold is **80% attendance per study period**. In a typical 10-week term with daily classes, this means missing more than **10 days** without approved leave can result in a formal warning and potential reporting.

### Can ANU cancel my student visa directly?
No. ANU does not have the authority to cancel visas. The university reports breaches through **PRISMS**, and only the **Department of Home Affairs** can issue a cancellation notice. However, once ANU lodges a report, the Department initiates its own assessment, which can result in visa cancellation if the breach is confirmed.

### What happens if I fail one course in a semester at ANU?
Failing a single course does not automatically breach visa conditions, but it may place you on a **course progress warning**. If you fail additional courses in the following semester and your cumulative completion rate falls below 50%, ANU will initiate the formal intervention and potential reporting process.

### How long does the ANU appeals process take for visa compliance matters?
Students have **20 working days** to lodge an internal appeal after receiving a notice of intention to report. ANU aims to resolve appeals within **10 working days** of receiving a complete submission, though complex cases may take longer. The university cannot report to Home Affairs while a valid appeal is pending.
