The Australian National University (ANU) received 21,962 undergraduate applications for the 2024 academic year, with an offer rate of approximately 72.5%, according to the university’s 2024 Annual Report. Among these, international student applications constituted a significant portion, and the personal statement remains a decisive factor in the competitive admissions process, particularly for programs with capped enrolment such as the Bachelor of Advanced Computing (Honours) and the Bachelor of Philosophy (Ph.D.) program. This article provides a structured guide to crafting a strong ANU undergraduate personal statement, covering required structure, key prompts from the official application portal, and annotated example essays that illustrate how to align personal narratives with ANU’s stated selection criteria.

## The Two-Part Structure of the ANU Personal Statement

The ANU undergraduate application system requires two distinct written components: a **Statement of Purpose** (maximum 500 words) and a **Personal Statement** (maximum 500 words). Each serves a different function in the selection process.

### Statement of Purpose: Academic Alignment

The Statement of Purpose must demonstrate a clear **academic rationale** for choosing a specific program at ANU. This section should include: (1) specific courses or research clusters within the chosen degree that align with the applicant’s academic interests; (2) named faculty members whose work the applicant has read or followed; and (3) how the program’s curriculum structure (e.g., compulsory research project, honours pathway) suits the applicant’s long-term goals. The ANU Admissions Office (2024) explicitly states that generic statements about “world-class education” are not weighted favourably.

### Personal Statement: Extracurricular and Contextual Evidence

The Personal Statement focuses on **non-academic attributes** such as leadership, resilience, community engagement, and cultural awareness. This section should provide concrete evidence—not claims—of these qualities. For example, rather than stating “I am a leader,” the applicant should describe a specific instance where they organised a team project or led a volunteer initiative, including measurable outcomes (e.g., number of participants, funds raised, or hours contributed). The Australian Government Department of Education (2023) notes that holistic admissions criteria increasingly value demonstrated competencies beyond academic grades.

## Key Prompts from the ANU Application Portal

ANU’s online application portal presents three mandatory prompts that applicants must address within the two 500-word statements.

### Prompt 1: Why This Program at ANU?

This prompt requires applicants to articulate a **specific academic fit** between their interests and the program’s offerings. Effective responses reference: (1) a named research centre, such as the Research School of Computer Science or the Crawford School of Public Policy; (2) a particular course code (e.g., COMP3620 Artificial Intelligence) and its relevance to the applicant’s prior project or reading; and (3) how the program’s teaching approach (e.g., problem-based learning, industry placement) matches the applicant’s learning style. The ANU Admissions Office (2024) advises against mentioning rankings or generic reputation without connecting them to personal academic goals.

### Prompt 2: Describe a Significant Challenge You Overcame

This prompt assesses **resilience and problem-solving** skills. Strong responses follow a STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) framework: describe the context, the specific obstacle, the steps taken to address it, and the measurable outcome. The Australian Bureau of Statistics (2023) reports that 47% of university students aged 18–24 work part-time while studying, making time-management challenges a common but acceptable topic if presented with concrete strategies and results.

### Prompt 3: How Have You Contributed to Your Community?

This prompt evaluates **civic engagement and cultural competence**. Evidence of community contribution can include volunteer work, school clubs, cultural events, or family responsibilities. The key is to demonstrate impact: quantify hours, participants, or outcomes. For international applicants, the Department of Home Affairs (2024) notes that community involvement is a positive factor in visa character assessments, though this should not be the primary motivation stated.

## Annotated Example Essay: Statement of Purpose

Below is an annotated example for a hypothetical applicant to the Bachelor of Advanced Computing (Honours) program.

**Example Statement of Purpose (500 words)**

“My academic interest in computational complexity theory began during a Year 11 mathematics project on the P vs NP problem, where I implemented a brute-force algorithm for the travelling salesman problem in Python. This experience led me to explore the work of Professor Edith Hemaspaandra at ANU’s Research School of Computer Science, particularly her research on computational social choice and election manipulation algorithms. Her 2022 paper ‘Complexity of Manipulative Voting’ directly addresses questions I have been investigating independently.

The Bachelor of Advanced Computing (Honours) program’s compulsory COMP3670 Research Project in the third year offers a structured pathway to conduct supervised research in this area. I have reviewed the course syllabus for COMP3620 Artificial Intelligence and note that its module on constraint satisfaction problems aligns with my prior work on optimisation algorithms. Additionally, the program’s industry placement component, which ANU reports places 92% of students in relevant internships, would allow me to apply theoretical knowledge to real-world problems.

My long-term goal is to pursue a Ph.D. in computational social choice, and the Honours year at ANU provides the required research training. I have already read three papers from the ANU Algorithms and Data Group and corresponded with a current PhD student about the group’s weekly seminars. This direct engagement confirms that ANU’s research environment matches my academic trajectory.”

**Annotation**: This essay demonstrates specific academic alignment (named professor, paper, course code), uses quantified data (92% placement rate), and connects prior experience to future goals without generic praise.

## Annotated Example Essay: Personal Statement

Below is an annotated example for the same hypothetical applicant.

**Example Personal Statement (500 words)**

“In Year 10, I founded a coding club at my high school with five members. Over two years, I recruited 28 students, organised 12 workshops on Python and web development, and secured AUD 1,200 in school funding for equipment. The club’s participants collectively completed three community projects: a website for a local animal shelter, a donation tracker for a food bank, and a scheduling tool for a volunteer tutoring program.

This experience required me to develop leadership skills through delegation and conflict resolution. When two club members disagreed on the project timeline, I facilitated a meeting where we created a shared Gantt chart, breaking tasks into two-week sprints. The project was completed on schedule, and the shelter’s website now receives an average of 200 visits per month.

Outside of school, I volunteer at a community centre teaching basic computer skills to elderly residents. Over 18 months, I have conducted 30 sessions, each attended by 8–12 participants. One participant, a 72-year-old retiree, now uses email independently to communicate with family overseas. This experience has taught me patience and the importance of adapting communication styles to different audiences.

Balancing these commitments with academic work required careful time management. I maintained a 3.8 GPA while dedicating 6–8 hours per week to the coding club and 2–3 hours to volunteering. I used a digital calendar to block study periods and completed homework during school breaks. This structure allowed me to meet all deadlines without compromising quality.”

**Annotation**: This essay provides specific numbers (28 members, AUD 1,200, 12 workshops, 30 sessions), concrete outcomes (200 monthly visits, independent email use), and a measurable GPA (3.8). It demonstrates leadership, community contribution, and time management without abstract claims.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Several recurring errors reduce the effectiveness of ANU personal statements, as noted by the ANU Admissions Office (2024) and observed in feedback from the Unilink Education applicant database (2025).

### Generic Opening Sentences

Phrases such as “I have always been passionate about science” or “From a young age, I dreamed of studying at ANU” are flagged as low-effort. The ANU Admissions Office (2024) advises starting with a **specific academic or personal experience** that directly relates to the chosen program. For example, “During a Year 11 biology dissection, I observed the structure of a frog’s heart and wondered how cardiac muscle regeneration could be induced in humans.”

### Overuse of Superlatives Without Evidence

Statements like “I am a hardworking and dedicated student” are not weighted without supporting evidence. Instead, applicants should **quantify or describe** the behaviour: “I maintained a 4.0 GPA while working 15 hours per week at a retail job, using a study schedule that allocated two hours each evening to revision.”

### Ignoring the Word Limit

The 500-word limit for each statement is strictly enforced. Submissions exceeding this limit may be truncated or rejected. Applicants should use a word counter tool and trim redundancies. The Australian Government Department of Education (2023) notes that adherence to submission guidelines is a basic indicator of organisational competence.

## FAQ

### Q1: Can I use the same personal statement for multiple Australian universities?

No. Each university has different prompts and word limits. ANU requires two separate 500-word statements with specific prompts. Using a generic statement from another university (e.g., UNSW or University of Melbourne) may result in rejection, as the ANU Admissions Office (2024) explicitly states that non-responsive content is not considered. Tailor each statement to the specific program and institution.

### Q2: Should I mention my ATAR or predicted IB score in the personal statement?

No. Academic grades are submitted separately through the application portal and are not part of the personal statement. The ANU Admissions Office (2024) advises against repeating grade information in the written components, as this wastes limited word count. Instead, focus on qualitative evidence of academic interest, leadership, and community contribution.

### Q3: How important is the personal statement compared to academic grades?

The weighting varies by program. For competitive programs like the Bachelor of Philosophy (Ph.D.) and Bachelor of Advanced Computing (Honours), the personal statement can account for up to 30% of the selection decision, according to ANU’s 2024 Admissions Policy. For programs with lower demand, grades are the primary factor. Applicants to competitive programs should allocate significant time to crafting a strong statement.
