Navigating the **ANU deferred examination** process can feel overwhelming, especially when you are already dealing with illness, personal hardship, or unexpected disruptions. The Australian National University enrolled over 22,000 students in 2025, and in Semester 1 alone, the examinations office processed more than 3,800 special consideration applications across all colleges. Understanding exactly what evidence you need—and what standards the university applies—is the difference between a successful deferral and a stressful rejection.

This guide examines the **ANU special consideration medical certificate** requirements in detail, explores valid **non-medical grounds** for exam deferral, and explains the most common **ANU exam deferral rejection reasons** you need to avoid. Whether you are applying through the College of Arts and Social Sciences or the College of Engineering, Computing and Cybernetics, the core evidence standards remain consistent, but the nuances matter.

---

## Understanding ANU Deferred Examinations and Special Consideration

The **ANU deferred exam application** process is governed by the university's Assessment Rule 2024, which came into full effect at the start of the 2025 academic year. Under this framework, students who cannot attend a scheduled examination due to circumstances beyond their control may apply for a deferred assessment. A deferred examination is not a guaranteed right—it is a privilege granted only when sufficient and verifiable evidence supports the claim.

The university distinguishes between a **deferred examination** (where a new exam date is set, typically within the deferred examination period two to four weeks after the original schedule) and **special consideration** (which may involve alternative assessment arrangements, such as a take-home paper or adjusted weighting). In many cases, students apply for both simultaneously through the same online portal. The critical factor for approval is the quality and specificity of the evidence submitted.

**Key deadlines** are non-negotiable. For most faculties, applications must be lodged within three working days of the original examination date. Late applications require an additional explanation for the delay itself, and approvals become significantly harder to obtain after the deadline has passed.

## Medical Certificate Standards for ANU Special Consideration

When illness or injury prevents you from sitting an exam, a **medical certificate** is the most common form of evidence. However, not all certificates meet the university's standards. The ANU special consideration medical certificate requirements are specific, and generic notes from a GP often fall short.

A valid medical certificate for ANU purposes must include **all** of the following elements:

- The date of consultation and the date the certificate was issued
- The specific dates on which you were unfit to sit the examination
- A clear statement that the practitioner considers you unfit to undertake the assessment
- The practitioner's name, provider number, and signature (or digital equivalent)
- The practice's official letterhead or stamp

The most common reason for rejection is a certificate that states the student "reported feeling unwell" without the practitioner's independent clinical judgment. The university requires the practitioner to confirm, based on their professional assessment, that you were genuinely incapacitated. A certificate dated two days after the exam with vague wording such as "was unwell last week" will almost certainly be rejected.

**Telehealth certificates** are accepted, provided they meet the same content standards. Since 2024, ANU has explicitly confirmed that online consultations are valid sources of medical evidence, but the certificate must still specify the dates of incapacity and the practitioner's clinical opinion.

## Non-Medical Grounds for ANU Exam Deferral

Medical conditions are not the only valid basis for an **ANU exam deferral non-medical grounds** application. The university recognises a range of circumstances that may significantly impair your ability to perform in an examination. Understanding these categories and their evidence requirements is essential for a successful application.

### Bereavement and Compassionate Grounds

The death or serious illness of a close family member is a recognised ground for deferral. Evidence typically includes a death certificate, funeral notice, or a letter from a medical professional regarding the family member's condition. The university generally considers parents, siblings, children, partners, and grandparents as close family members. For extended family, you may need to provide additional context explaining the closeness of the relationship.

### Personal Trauma or Victimisation

Incidents involving assault, domestic violence, or significant personal safety concerns are treated with appropriate sensitivity. Evidence can include a police report, a letter from a counsellor or support service, or a statutory declaration. ANU has a dedicated Student Safety and Wellbeing team that can assist with documentation and liaise with your college on your behalf.

### Unforeseeable Technical Failures

For online or remotely proctored examinations, a documented technical failure may constitute valid grounds. This requires evidence such as screenshots with timestamps, error logs, or a report from ANU IT Services confirming the outage. Simply stating that your internet disconnected is insufficient without corroborating proof. The university checks all technical failure claims against its own system logs.

### Elite Athlete or Defence Commitments

Students representing Australia in recognised sporting competitions or those with Australian Defence Force reserve obligations may apply for deferral on these grounds. Supporting evidence must come from the official sporting body or ADF unit, specifying the dates of the commitment and confirming that attendance was mandatory.

## ANU Special Exam Evidence Requirements by Category

Meeting the **ANU special exam evidence requirements** means understanding what each category demands. The following table summarises the key evidence types required for common deferral applications:

| Ground for Deferral | Required Evidence | Common Pitfalls |
|---|---|---|
| Medical (physical illness) | Medical certificate with specific dates and clinical opinion | Certificate lacking practitioner's independent assessment |
| Medical (mental health) | Letter from psychologist, psychiatrist, or GP with mental health treatment history | Generic stress or exam anxiety without clinical diagnosis |
| Bereavement | Death certificate, funeral notice, or statutory declaration | Insufficient detail on relationship to deceased |
| Personal trauma | Police report, counsellor letter, or statutory declaration | Delayed reporting without explanation |
| Technical failure | Screenshots, error logs, IT Services confirmation | No timestamped evidence |
| Elite sport / ADF | Official letter from organisation with dates | Letter not on official letterhead |

**Statutory declarations** are accepted for most non-medical grounds but carry less weight than third-party documentation. If you rely on a statutory declaration, it must be detailed, specific about dates and impacts, and ideally supported by at least one additional piece of corroborating evidence.

## ANU Exam Deferral Rejection Reasons and How to Avoid Them

Understanding the most frequent **ANU exam deferral rejection reasons** can help you prepare a stronger application. The examinations office and college assessors consistently cite several recurring issues that lead to denial.

**Insufficient medical detail** is the single most common reason for rejection. A certificate that says "John was seen today and reports feeling unwell" does not demonstrate that the practitioner formed an independent view that you were incapacitated. Always ask your doctor to explicitly state that, in their clinical judgment, you were unfit to sit the examination on the specified date.

**Late applications without explanation** are routinely denied. The three-working-day rule is strict, and while extensions are possible in exceptional circumstances, you must explain why you could not apply within the deadline. Being too unwell to complete the form is itself a valid reason, but you need to state this explicitly and provide evidence of ongoing incapacity.

**Mismatch between evidence and claimed dates** is another frequent issue. If your medical certificate covers Monday but your exam was on Wednesday, the assessor will question whether you could have recovered by then. The evidence must directly cover the date of the examination.

**Repeated applications for the same condition** can attract scrutiny. If you have applied for deferred examinations in multiple semesters citing the same ongoing condition, the university may ask why you have not sought a longer-term adjustment through Access and Inclusion. A pattern of deferrals without a documented management plan can lead to a request for additional documentation or even referral to academic progress review.

**Employment-related applications** are almost never approved. ANU policy explicitly states that work commitments, including shifts that conflict with an exam schedule, do not constitute grounds for special consideration. Students are expected to arrange their employment around the published examination timetable.

**Misunderstanding of "special consideration" scope** also leads to disappointment. Special consideration is for circumstances that significantly impair your performance or prevent attendance. Minor illnesses, common colds without significant symptoms, or feeling "underprepared" do not qualify. The bar is set at a level of disruption that a reasonable person would consider serious.

## Step-by-Step Guide to a Successful ANU Deferred Exam Application

Preparing a strong **ANU deferred exam application** requires attention to detail and prompt action. Follow these steps to maximise your chances of approval.

**Step 1: Assess your eligibility immediately.** As soon as you realise you cannot attend the exam, determine whether your circumstances fit within the recognised grounds. If you are unsure, contact your college's student administration team for preliminary advice before the exam date if possible.

**Step 2: Gather evidence promptly.** For medical conditions, see a doctor on or before the exam day if you can. A certificate obtained on the day of the exam carries significantly more weight than one sought retrospectively. For non-medical grounds, collect documentation as soon as it becomes available.

**Step 3: Complete the online application through ISIS.** The application requires you to select the course and examination, specify the grounds, and upload your evidence. Provide a clear, concise personal statement explaining how the circumstances affected your ability to sit the exam. Avoid exaggeration—assessors are trained to identify inconsistencies.

**Step 4: Submit within three working days.** The clock starts on the day of the original examination. If you are hospitalised or otherwise unable to meet this deadline, have someone contact your college on your behalf if possible, and submit as soon as you are able with an explanation for the delay.

**Step 5: Monitor your application status.** Decisions are typically communicated within ten working days. If additional evidence is requested, provide it promptly. A request for further information is not a rejection—it is an opportunity to strengthen your case.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I apply for an ANU deferred exam if my medical certificate is dated after the exam day?**
A: Yes, but the certificate must clearly state that the practitioner assessed you as unfit for the specific date of the examination, based on their clinical judgment. A retrospective certificate dated 27 May 2026 for an exam on 23 May 2026 is acceptable only if the doctor explicitly confirms you were incapacitated on 23 May. Certificates that merely record your reported symptoms without clinical assessment will not meet the standard.

**Q: What happens if my ANU special consideration application is rejected? Can I appeal?**
A: You have the right to request a review of the decision within 10 working days of receiving the rejection notice. Reviews are conducted by a different assessor, usually at the Associate Dean level. You may submit additional evidence during the review process. In 2025, approximately 18% of rejected applications were overturned on review, often because students provided more detailed medical documentation or clarified the impact of their circumstances.

**Q: Are mental health conditions treated differently from physical illnesses for ANU exam deferral purposes?**
A: No. The university's policy does not distinguish between physical and mental health conditions. However, the evidence requirements can be more nuanced. A letter from a psychologist or psychiatrist should specify the diagnosis (if appropriate), the dates of impact, and a clinical opinion on your fitness to sit the examination. If you have an existing mental health condition, it is strongly recommended that you also register with ANU Access and Inclusion for ongoing support, as repeated deferrals for the same condition may prompt questions about whether reasonable adjustments could prevent future crises.

**Q: How many times can I defer the same ANU examination?**
A: There is no fixed numerical limit, but each application is assessed on its merits. If you defer the same course's examination in Semester 1 2026 and again in Semester 2 2026, the assessor will closely examine whether the circumstances are genuinely new and unforeseeable. The university may require you to meet with an academic advisor or Access and Inclusion to develop a plan before approving a third deferral. Students who accumulate multiple deferred examinations may also face restrictions on enrolling in subsequent courses until outstanding assessments are completed.
