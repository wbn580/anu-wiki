The Australian National University's **Master of Public Policy (MPP)** at the Crawford School of Public Policy has long been recognised as a gateway to influential careers in government, international organisations, and the non-profit sector. With over 85% of graduates securing policy-related roles within six months of completion according to the 2026 Graduate Outcomes Survey, the program's internship component plays a decisive role in shaping employability. The **ANU Master of Public Policy internship** is not merely an add-on; it is an academically integrated experience that bridges theoretical frameworks with real-world policy challenges. Students gain direct exposure to the machinery of government, legislative processes, and evidence-based decision-making while earning **ANU postgraduate internship credit** toward their degree. This guide unpacks every dimension of the **Crawford School placement process**, from host matching to assessment criteria, and examines the tangible career outcomes that follow.

## Understanding the Role of Internships in the ANU MPP Curriculum

The ANU Master of Public Policy is a two-year full-time program requiring completion of 96 units. Within this structure, students may undertake an internship as part of their elective coursework, typically through the **Policy Practice** elective stream. The internship is designed to provide immersive professional experience in a policy environment, allowing students to apply analytical skills acquired in core units such as Public Policy Analysis, Governance and Public Sector Reform, and Economic Policy.

**ANU postgraduate internship credit** is awarded upon successful completion of a placement that meets academic and professional standards. Most internships carry 6 to 12 units of credit, depending on the duration and scope of the project. A standard 6-unit placement generally requires a minimum of 120 hours of supervised work, while a 12-unit internship extends to 240 hours or more. The Crawford School emphasises that internships must involve substantive policy tasks—research, briefing, evaluation, or stakeholder engagement—rather than administrative support. This ensures that the experience aligns with the program's learning outcomes and contributes meaningfully to students' professional development.

The internship is assessed through a combination of a reflective journal, a policy portfolio, and a supervisor evaluation. These components collectively demonstrate the student's ability to integrate academic knowledge with practical policy work. The academic convenor reviews each submission against criteria including analytical rigour, professional communication, and ethical awareness. **ANU MPP career outcomes** data from 2026 indicates that students who completed an internship during their degree were 40% more likely to receive a job offer before graduation compared to those who did not.

## The Crawford School Placement Process: Step by Step

The **Crawford School placement process** is deliberately structured to support students from initial inquiry through to final assessment. Understanding this sequence is essential for maximising the likelihood of securing a placement that aligns with career aspirations.

**Step 1: Eligibility and Preparation Workshop**
Before applying, students must attend a mandatory internship preparation workshop offered each semester. This session covers resume tailoring, professional correspondence, workplace expectations, and an overview of available host organisations. Academic staff also clarify the credit structure and assessment requirements. Students must have completed at least 24 units of coursework with a minimum GPA of 5.0 to be eligible for a credited internship.

**Step 2: Expression of Interest and Host Matching**
Students submit an expression of interest detailing their policy areas of interest, preferred work mode, and any geographic constraints. The Crawford School's **ANU public policy internship hosts** database includes over 60 organisations spanning federal departments, state agencies, think tanks, multilateral bodies, and advocacy groups. The placement coordinator then facilitates introductions based on alignment between student profiles and host needs. While the school provides strong facilitation, placement is not guaranteed; hosts make final selection decisions.

**Step 3: Interview and Selection**
Host organisations typically conduct a brief interview to assess the candidate's suitability. Students are encouraged to prepare by researching the host's policy portfolio and recent publications. The selection process is competitive, particularly for placements at high-profile institutions such as the Department of the Prime Minister and Cabinet, the Productivity Commission, and international organisations based in Canberra.

**Step 4: Learning Agreement and Supervision**
Once selected, the student, host supervisor, and academic convenor sign a learning agreement outlining project objectives, deliverables, and a schedule of supervisory meetings. This document ensures mutual accountability and provides a reference point for mid-placement reviews. Hosts are expected to provide a dedicated supervisor with relevant policy expertise.

**Step 5: Mid-Placement Check-in and Final Assessment**
At the midpoint, students submit a brief progress report. At the conclusion, the host supervisor completes an evaluation form, and the student submits the reflective journal and policy portfolio. The academic convenor moderates all assessments to ensure consistency.

## Key Host Organisations and Placement Settings

The diversity of **ANU public policy internship hosts** reflects the breadth of the policy landscape in Australia and the Asia-Pacific region. Placements are concentrated in Canberra, leveraging the university's proximity to Parliament House, government departments, and diplomatic missions. However, remote and hybrid arrangements have become increasingly common since 2025, expanding access for students with caring responsibilities or mobility constraints.

**Federal Government Departments**
Departments such as the Department of Social Services, the Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water, and the Treasury regularly host MPP interns. Typical projects include contributing to policy submissions, analysing consultation feedback, and drafting ministerial briefs. These placements offer unparalleled insight into the policy cycle and the interplay between political and bureaucratic actors.

**Regulatory Agencies and Statutory Bodies**
Organisations like the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission and the Australian Energy Regulator provide exposure to regulatory policy design and enforcement. Interns may assist with market studies, compliance reviews, or stakeholder engagement strategies.

**Think Tanks and Research Institutes**
The Grattan Institute, the Australia Institute, and the Lowy Institute are among the non-government hosts that welcome ANU MPP students. These placements often involve data analysis, literature reviews, and co-authoring public-facing reports. The emphasis on evidence-based advocacy sharpens skills valued in both public and private sectors.

**International Organisations and NGOs**
Canberra's diplomatic precinct hosts missions from over 100 countries, and organisations such as the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees and the International Organization for Migration maintain a presence. Internships in these settings frequently address transnational policy challenges, including migration governance, climate adaptation, and development cooperation.

**State and Territory Agencies**
For students interested in service delivery and intergovernmental relations, placements with ACT Government directorates or cross-jurisdictional bodies offer a complementary perspective to federal policy work.

The 2026 host database reflects a deliberate effort to include organisations working on Indigenous policy, gender equality, and digital transformation—areas identified as priority growth sectors in the Australian Public Service Workforce Strategy 2025–2030.

## Academic Integration and Assessment of the Internship

The internship is not merely work experience; it is a **credit-bearing academic unit** with rigorous intellectual demands. The Crawford School has designed the assessment structure to deepen students' capacity for critical reflection and professional communication—competencies that underpin **ANU MPP career outcomes**.

The **reflective journal** requires weekly entries that connect workplace experiences to theoretical concepts encountered in the MPP curriculum. A student working on housing policy, for example, might analyse how path dependency theory explains resistance to zoning reform observed during their placement. This exercise cultivates the habit of linking practice to scholarship, a hallmark of effective policy professionals.

The **policy portfolio** is a curated collection of work produced during the internship, accompanied by a framing essay. The portfolio might include a research brief, a stakeholder mapping exercise, and a draft submission. The framing essay contextualises these artifacts within the host organisation's mission and the broader policy environment. Students must demonstrate not only technical competence but also an understanding of the political and ethical dimensions of their work.

The **supervisor evaluation** accounts for a portion of the final grade and assesses professional attributes such as reliability, initiative, and interpersonal effectiveness. Host supervisors use a standardised rubric developed by the Crawford School.

Together, these assessments ensure that the **ANU Master of Public Policy internship** is a transformative educational experience rather than a transactional arrangement. The academic convenor provides feedback that helps students articulate their learning in job applications and interviews, directly enhancing employability.

## Strategic Considerations for Securing a Preferred Placement

Given the competitive nature of the **Crawford School placement process**, students benefit from a proactive and strategic approach. The following considerations are distilled from advice provided by the Crawford School careers team and feedback from host supervisors.

**Clarify Policy Interests Early**
Students who articulate a clear but flexible policy focus—such as health economics, environmental governance, or digital regulation—are better positioned to identify suitable hosts. The Crawford School encourages students to use their first semester to explore interests through elective units and public seminars before committing to an internship application.

**Build a Professional Profile**
Host organisations assess candidates on both academic achievement and professional presentation. A well-structured resume highlighting research skills, data analysis proficiency, and relevant volunteer or work experience is essential. The Crawford School offers resume review services and mock interviews specifically tailored to policy roles.

**Engage with the Policy Community**
Attending public lectures, parliamentary committee hearings, and policy networking events in Canberra helps students understand the landscape and make informal connections. Many host supervisors report that they prefer candidates who have demonstrated genuine interest by attending events or engaging with the organisation's work prior to applying.

**Be Geographically Flexible**
While most placements are in Canberra, students willing to undertake placements in other Australian cities or remotely may access a wider range of opportunities. The 2026 host database includes organisations in Sydney, Melbourne, and Brisbane, as well as virtual placements with international bodies.

**Prepare for the Academic Demands**
The internship's academic component requires consistent effort throughout the placement period. Students who fall behind on reflective journal entries often struggle to produce a high-quality policy portfolio. Time management and disciplined writing habits are critical.
